### 2.1 Introduction to ITER and Magnetic Confinement
Magnetic confinement fusion, specifically within the ITER tokamak architecture, represents humanity's most ambitious endeavor to harness the power of the stars. The goal is to confine a plasma of deuterium and tritium isotopes at temperatures exceeding 150 million degrees Celsius, using immensely powerful, superconducting magnetic fields. 

### 2.2 The Computational and Physical Bottlenecks
While the theoretical physics of fusion have been understood for decades, realizing a net-positive energy reactor (Q > 10) is fundamentally gated by our ability to predict and control the nonlinear dynamics of the plasma. The Phase III Autoresearch protocols targeted the four most severe computational and operational bottlenecks in fusion physics:
- **The "Curse of Dimensionality"**: Solving 6D kinetic turbulence equations requires Exascale computing.
- **Thermal Quenches**: Catastrophic loss of confinement that can vaporize the reactor's first wall.
- **Divertor Structural Failure**: Edge Localized Modes (ELMs) destroying solid-state plasma-facing components.
- **Real-Time Control Latency**: The inability to solve complex Partial Differential Equations (PDEs) fast enough (sub-millisecond) to actuate magnetic containment coils.
