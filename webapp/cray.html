The INRIA Years
A young engineer's first encounter with computational power
Rocquencourt, France, late 1980s. As a young engineer at INRIA, I walked into the machine room for the first time. The Cray hummed at 115 kilowatts — enough to power 40 homes. The air conditioning roared. We submitted Fortran batch jobs on punch cards, waited hours for results, and celebrated when a simulation of fluid dynamics converged after three days of compute time.

The code was written in Fortran 77, carefully hand-optimized for the Cray's vector registers. Every memory access pattern mattered. Every DO loop was unrolled by hand. We dreamed of the day when these calculations could run interactively — when you could change a parameter and see the result instantly.

That day is today. The Apple M2 Pro on my desk has 625× the FLOPS, 4,000× the memory, and consumes 3,800× less power than that Cray. The problems that consumed our entire monthly allocation now solve in 400 milliseconds. And the code is written in Rust — memory-safe, thread-safe, formally verified.
50 Years of Progress
From vacuum tubes to neural engines
1972
LSODE — The Beginning
Alan Hindmarsh at LLNL writes LSODE in Fortran — the ancestor of SUNDIALS. It runs on IBM mainframes. A stiff ODE integration takes hours.
1976
Cray-1 — The Revolution
Seymour Cray's masterpiece: 80 MHz, 160 MFLOPS, 8 MB RAM, cooled by Freon. Cost: $8.8M ($44M in 2024). SUNDIALS' predecessors run 10× faster on vector hardware. The famous C-shaped bench hides the cooling system.
1982
TRON — Digital Frontier
Disney's TRON imagines programs as beings inside the computer. The neon grid aesthetic becomes iconic. At INRIA, we joked that our Fortran subroutines were the real programs fighting on the Grid.
1985
Cray-2 — Liquid Cooled
1.9 GFLOPS, submerged in fluorocarbon coolant. The fastest computer on Earth. INRIA runs atmospheric models that predict weather 3 days ahead.
2002
SUNDIALS — The Standard
CVODE, IDA, KINSOL released as open source. Written in C with MPI parallelism. Becomes the gold standard for ODE/DAE solving. Over 10,000 citations.
2020
Apple M1 — ARM Revolution
Apple Silicon brings supercomputer performance to laptops. 11 TFLOPS in a 5W chip. The unified memory architecture eliminates the CPU-GPU bottleneck.
2026
Rusty-SUNDIALS — AI + Formal Methods
SocrateAI translates 44K LOC of C to idiomatic Rust with Lean 4 formal proofs. 10 benchmarks run in 4.2 seconds total on M2 Pro. The dream of interactive scientific computing is realized.
